Introduction¶

A/B tests are very commonly performed by data analysts and data scientists. It is important to get some practice working with the difficulties of these

For this project, I will perform an A/B test for an e-commerce website. The goal is to help the company understand if they should implement the new page, keep the old page, or perhaps run the experiment longer to make their decision.

Part II - A/B Test¶

1. Consider Type I error rate of 5%, based on all the data provided, this test will try to find out if there is significant evidence to conclure that the new page is better than the old one (that means the new page is efficient) ? To do that, let's start with a hypothesis ($H_{0}$) which is the reverse belief - the new page is worst than the old one

  • $H_{0}$: $p_{new}$ - $p_{old}$ <= 0
  • $H_{1}$: $p_{new}$ - $p_{old}$ > 0

2. Steps:

  • Assume under the null hypothesis, $p_{new}$ and $p_{old}$ both have "true" success rates equal to the converted success rate regardless of page - that is $p_{new}$ and $p_{old}$ are equal. Furthermore, assume they are equal to the converted rate in ab_data.csv regardless of the page.

  • Use a sample size for each page equal to the ones in ab_data.csv.

  • Perform the sampling distribution for the difference in converted between the two pages over 10,000 iterations of calculating an estimate from the null.

a. The conversion rate for $p_{new}$ under the null

h. Create 10,000 $p_{new}$ - $p_{old}$ values using the same simulation process above. Store all 10,000 values in p_diffs.

In [148]:
p_diffs = []
olds = []
news = []
for _ in range(10000):
    new_page = np.random.binomial(1, p_new, n_new)
    old_page = np.random.binomial(1, p_old, n_old)
    olds.append(old_page.mean()), news.append(new_page.mean())
    p_diffs.append(new_page.mean() - old_page.mean())

  • In this case, the p_value is significant, and much greater than the acceptable error rate 0.05 --> We have to accept the hypothesis. That means the new page doesn't improve the efficiency. The company should not launch the new page.

k. Explain the meaning of z_score and p_value

  • Z-score is measure of standard deviation and p-value is probabilities of rejecting the null hypothesis. Practically, a very high or a very low (negative) Z-score demonstrates that almost observations are far from the 95% confidence interval of the null-hypothesis. That means, it associates with a very small p-value and indicates that there is no evidence to support the null hypothesis.
z-score (Standard Deviations) p-value (Probability) Confidence level
< -1.65 or > +1.65 < 0.10 90%
< -1.96 or > +1.96 < 0.05 95%
< -2.58 or > +2.58 < 0.01 99%
  • The p_value in this part (0.905) is similar to the finding in part j (0.9074). The z-score in this case is not very negative (-1.31). So, again, it's support the conclusion of p_value: accepting the null hypothesis

Conclusion¶

  • This calculation gives us an interpretation for the Canada and the new page. Holding all other variables constant, a user from Canada uses the new page would be about 1.08 times more likely to convert. However, the p_value is quite big (~ 0.05), so, it isn't really practical significance. Again, we do not have enough evidence to reject the null hypothesis based on any of our A/B testing.

  • As a result, there is no reason to switch to the new page, when the old one performs just as well.
